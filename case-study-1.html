Improving Learnability and Mitigating Cybersickness in Tactical VR

AimXR is a complex spatial VR First-Person Shooter that demands high cognitive load and fine-motor coordination. Initial heuristic reviews indicated that the game's spatial UI clustering and opaque virtual physics were causing severe usability friction and cybersickness among novice players.

You can explore the official game overview and introductory trailer on the AimXR page on Meta Quest.

If you want the full research write‑up and artifacts, you can read the detailed project report here (Google Doc).

01 — Executive Summary

Executive Summary

The Problem

AimXR's spatial UI clustering and opaque virtual physics were causing severe usability friction and cybersickness among novice players.

My Role

Worked in a team of two. I moderated usability testing for half the participants (n = 4) and took sole ownership of data synthesis: video session analysis, thematic coding, and descriptive statistical analysis.

The Impact

Identified 4 critical heuristic failures (including Fitts's Law violations and mental model collapse) and proposed data-driven redesigns to enhance interaction efficiency, unify mental models, and reduce vestibular mismatch.

Qualitative

Conducted thematic analysis of Post-Task Questionnaires and stitched video observation (headset recording + physical body movement) to analyze hesitation and physical struggle.

03 — Data Analysis & Key Findings

What the Data Revealed

Triangulating GEQ/VRSQ deltas with observational themes revealed four system-level usability failures:

Finding 1: Fitts's Law Violations

37.5% of participants (3/8) reported clutter & mis-grabs

Overlapping belt hitboxes caused frequent mis-grabs that broke flow.

Finding 2: Mental Model Collapse

Task abandonment observed (Participant 8)

Inconsistent throwable interactions fractured expectations and drove abandonment.

Finding 3: Physics Opacity

Immersion delta: −0.83 (Participant 7)

Opaque virtual physics made “correct” throws feel unpredictable.

Finding 4: Vestibular Mismatch

83.33% initial Oculomotor strain (Participant 8)

Comfort vignette didn’t protect users during dynamic motion (e.g., turning/strafing).

04 — Proposed Data-Driven Solutions

Translating Findings into Redesigns

Solution 1: Dynamic Spatial Buffering

Increase spatial padding between belt inventory slots by 20%. Integrate "Gaze-Assisted Hitboxes" — if a user looks at a target but reaches for their belt, the system slightly expands the collision volume of their primary weapon to forgive blind, millimeter-level grabbing inaccuracies.

Solution 2: Standardized Interaction Models

Unify all throwables to a one-handed, gross-motor interaction model (e.g., pre-lit Molotovs, "teeth-pull" affordance for flashbangs) to reduce cognitive load.

Solution 3: Post-Throw Trajectory Visualization

Render a diegetic, dashed trajectory arc immediately upon release of a throwable.

This bridges the kinesthetic disconnect by instantly showing the object's path, providing the immediate feedback loop novices need to calibrate their physical effort for the next throw.

Solution 4: Omni-Directional Vignetting

Decouple the comfort vignette from strictly forward movement. Trigger FOV restriction during any artificial translation or rotation (strafing, falling, snap-turning).

Scaling opacity based on camera speed protects the user's vestibular system and mitigates "phantom falling".

05 — Limitations & Future Research

Limitations & Future Research

Sample Size Constraints

Testing was limited to 8 VR novices in a highly controlled, isolated environment. Real‑world physical fatigue over longer sessions was not measured.

Next Steps

Gaze‑Assisted Hitbox and Omni‑Directional Vignetting are data‑driven theories. Next, prototype them and run A/B tests against the original UI to mathematically validate error reduction.
